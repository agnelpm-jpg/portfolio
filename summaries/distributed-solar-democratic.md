# Distributed Solar Infrastructure And Democratic Governance

## Abstract

This paper extends MET into a full urban-energy transition model for Mumbai. It combines distributed rooftop solar, MVDC microgrid corridors, battery/storage coordination, commodity-linked settlement, and participatory governance.

## Core Thesis

The energy transition is not only an engineering problem. It is a coordination problem across infrastructure, settlement, regulation, and public trust.

\[
\text{Solar Infrastructure} + \text{Settlement Layer} + \text{Governance Layer}
\Rightarrow \text{Deployable Urban Energy Transition}
\]

## Technical Proposal

Mumbai rooftops are coordinated through interconnected microgrids and MVDC corridors. Generation is metered, routed, and settled without pretending that financial units are physical electricity.

\[
\text{Rooftop Solar} \rightarrow \text{MVDC Corridors} \rightarrow \text{Metered Output} \rightarrow \text{MET Settlement}
\]

## Economic Claim

The model targets a materially lower levelized cost of energy:

\[
\mathrm{LCOE} \approx \frac{\mathrm{CapEx}+\mathrm{OpEx}}{\mathrm{Lifetime\ Energy}} \approx \text{₹2.86}/\mathrm{kWh}
\]

The MET price corridor is proposed as:

\[
\text{₹2.86} \le P_{\mathrm{MET}} \le \text{₹7.50}
\]

with an operating band near:

\[
\text{₹3.36} \le P_{\mathrm{MET}} \le \text{₹6.50}
\]

## Governance Layer

The system separates physical electrons, accounting units, market instruments, and democratic oversight. That separation is the point: technical deployment without governance simply relocates risk.

## Essence

The proposal is a blueprint for making rooftop solar legible, financeable, and politically governable at city scale.
