MET: A Commodity-Linked Settlement Framework for Distributed Urban Solar Deployment in Mumbai 

## Abstract 

Mumbai’s electricity supply is predominantly coal-based via the state grid, yielding stationary energy emissions of ~16.9 MtCO₂e (2019) within a total city emissions profile of ~24–26 MtCO₂e. The city’s rooftop solar potential is estimated at ~1.7–2.1 GW, yet deployment remains constrained by fragmented incentives, delayed municipal payments, and the absence of market mechanisms for household and commercial prosumers. This paper proposes MET (Mumbai Energy Token/Unit) as a commodity-linked settlement instrument backed by verified electricity output from distributed solar assets and coordinated via a Tripartite Escrow Settlement System. MET enables a financing and settlement pathway that accommodates distributed generation, retail prosumer participation, and wholesale hedging via regulated commodity markets. The work integrates 
(1) distributed solar infrastructure, 
(2) escrow-based settlement, and 
(3) commodity-linked coordination, within an incremental regulatory pathway aligned with existing SEBI, MCX, RBI and MERC frameworks. The paper is a conceptual policy-technical whitepaper, not an engineering feasibility or regulatory filing. 

--- 

## 1. Introduction 

Mumbai’s energy supply is coal-intensive and centralized. Distributed solar deployment remains below technical potential due to structural frictions spanning 
(1) capital formation
(2) payment assurance
(3) tariff rigidity and 
(4) fragmented incentives. 
Traditional net-metering regimes do not produce meaningful prosumer participation at scale, nor do they enable municipal or district-level emission reduction pathways within the 2030-2050 climate targets outlined in MCAP and BMC climate budgeting frameworks. 

The framework in this paper does not replace the grid; it adds a coordination and settlement layer to make distributed solar economically deployable. 

--- 

## 2. Problem Structure 

Barriers to distributed solar adoption are multi-domain: 

## Technical: 

- fragmentation of rooftop capacity 

- absence of grid-integrated load balancing 

- battery deployment for peak shaving vs full firming 

- lack of metered verification standards 

Financial: 

- long EPC payment cycles (60–120 days) 

- absence of hedging instruments 

- no collateralizable revenue streams 

- weak prosumer incentives 

Regulatory: 

- tariff rigidity 

- licensing asymmetry between wholesale vs retail supply 

- unclear classification for prosumer settlements 

Governance: 

- corruption and payment leakage risks 

- insufficient municipal financial bandwidth 

- no public auditability 

These frictions suppress deployment despite feasible solar irradiance and viable technical potential. 

--- 

## 3. Solar & Storage Infrastructure Layer 

Mumbai’s rooftop technical potential is ~1.7–2.1 GW (MCAP). Distributed deployment requires: 

- rooftop PV assets (residential + commercial) 

- district-level MVDC corridors for collection/transmission 

- micro-battery and grid battery for peak shaving + settlement smoothing 

- smart metering for verification 

- grid interconnection for reliability 

## Important Clarification: 

Battery storage is sized for localized peak shaving, ramp smoothing, and settlement stability, not for full firming of peak demand. Full firming would require multi-GW/GWh storage not assumed here. 

--- 

## 4. MET Unit Definition 

MET is defined as a standardized unit of settlement exposure backed by verified electricity output. 

## Definition: 

1 MET = 1 kWh of verified generated electricity delivered to settlement. 

MET is not a currency, not a crypto-asset, and not a security representing ownership of physical electrons. It is a commodity-linked exposure unit analogous to standardized units in regulated commodity derivatives markets. 

--- 

5. Tripartite Escrow Settlement 

Settlement utilizes a Tripartite Escrow System: 

Parties: 

1. Payer (consumer, utility, commercial buyer) 

2. Escrow agent (RBI-regulated bank or payment institution) 

3. Payee (solar producer or EPC/asset operator) 

## Flow: 

- buyer deposits funds into escrow 

- smart meters + verification certify delivered energy 

- escrow releases payment on delivery (T+30) 

- disputes pause release, not custody 

- auditors verify throughput 

This removes municipal payment risk and compresses EPC receivables cycles from 60–120 days to ~30 days. It also produces collateralizable revenue streams for financing. 

--- 

6. Regulatory & Compliance Pathways 

The framework aligns with existing structures rather than creating new regulatory categories. 

Relevant pathways: 

- SEBI — commodity derivatives (electricity futures cash-settled model) 

- MCX — electricity futures precedent (post-2025 launch) 

- RBI — settlement via licensed payment institutions (CBDC optional) 

- MERC — tariff, distributed supply, and interconnection approvals 

- CERC — interoperability with national market standards 

CBDC is optional; settlement can be executed via standard payments infrastructure.

## 7. Financial Model Logic (Conceptual) 

Value stack consists of: 

- energy wholesale value (kWh) 

- hedging value (via futures) 

- collateral value (via escrow receivables) 

- participation value (prosumer/municipal) 

Revenue arithmetic requirement: Annual revenue = (annual verified generation) × (realized wholesale price) 

Numbers must remain internally consistent (no order-of-magnitude mismatch). 

--- 

## 8. MET Markets & Instrument Role 

MET units can interact with markets through: 

- primary issuance (via distributed generation contracts) 

- secondary trading (via regulated exchanges) 

- hedging (using electricity futures) 

MET is not intended for consumer payments; it is a coordination + settlement exposure instrument connecting distributed supply to commodity pricing. 

--- 

9. Governance Layer 

Governance requires: 

- public auditability 

- third-party verification 

- conflict-of-interest constraints 

- transparent procurement 

- anti-corruption controls 

No assumption of “democratic veto”; structure utilizes blocking minorities in supermajority processes.

## 10. Expansion Path (Optional) 

If successful at municipal scale: 

- regional aggregation (district/state) 

- cross-city coordination 

- multi-asset integration (BET) 

This is future work, not part of Mumbai deployment. 

--- 

## 11. Limitations 

This framework is not an EPC feasibility study. Exclusions include: 

- detailed load flow engineering 

- full legal filings 

- DSCR-grade bank financing structures 

- epidemiological health modeling 

- environmental impact assessments 

Regulatory approval timelines are indicative, not guaranteed. Adoption rates are not modeled. Storage sizing is not full firming. 

--- 

## 12. Conclusion 

Distributed solar potential in Mumbai is materially underutilized due to structural constraints rather than technical infeasibility. MET provides a coordination and settlement mechanism to make distributed deployments financeable and operational at scale without requiring crypto assets or new monetary categories. The framework operates within existing regulatory corridors and produces commodity-linked exposure rather than retail payments infrastructure.
