## **EXECUTIVE SUMMARY** 

Mumbai faces a critical energy paradox: peak electricity demand exceeds 4,000 MW while the city possesses approximately 1,724 MW of untapped rooftop solar potential—enough to supply roughly 50% of peak demand. Yet conventional renewable energy deployment remains trapped within centralized utility monopolies. 

This whitepaper proposes an integrated framework addressing three interconnected challenges through: 

1. Technical Innovation: Interconnected solar microgrids with MVDC transmission and distributed battery storage 

2. Regulatory Innovation: Energy-backed commodity settlement instruments (MET) structured as MCX electricity futures 

3. Governance Innovation: Participatory 21-member coordination committees with civil society majorities

- Participants: 555,000+ households and commercial establishments 

- Annual Revenue: ₹450–600 crore 

→ Implementation Timeline: 18-month pilot (2026–2027) National scaling (300 GW) → BRICS integration ($5–10 trillion market)

## **1. INTRODUCTION** 

## **1.1 The Energy Paradox** 

Mumbai, India's financial capital and most populous metropolitan region, confronts a paradox that defines 21st-century urban energy systems. 

## The Challenge: 

- Peak electricity demand: 4,000+ MW (2024–2025) 

- Annual growth rate: 5–8% 

- Coal dependence: 65% of electricity supply 

- Annual CO₂ emissions (energy sector): 1.65 million tonnes 

- Air pollution deaths annually: 15,000–20,000 

- Health system costs: ₹5,000 crore/year 

## The Opportunity: 

- Solar irradiance: 5–5.5 kWh/m²/day (among India's highest) 

- Rooftop solar potential: 1,724 MW 

- Distribution: 500,000+ households, 2,000+ commercial, 50+ industrial 

- Potential supply coverage: ~50% of peak demand 

## **1.2 Research Objective** 

This research investigates whether Mumbai can transition from a 20th-century centralized utility model to a 21st-century decentralized, democratized energy 

system while maintaining technical reliability, financial viability, and regulatory compliance. 

Core Thesis: Such a transition is technologically feasible, economically rational, and politically achievable through three complementary innovations.
